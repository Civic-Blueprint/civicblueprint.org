# Opening a childcare center in Denver, Colorado

The actual approvals you need, in the order they actually happen.

This is a working one-pager about how childcare-center licensing actually flows in one real city — Denver. It uses Denver only because Denver publishes most of the steps in primary sources. The shape of the flow (state license + parallel city approvals + a sequencing trap at the end) is similar in most US cities; the names and forms are what change.

Last reviewed against published sources: April 2026.

---

## If you'd rather skim, start here

Three short framings of the same artifact, depending on who is reading. Pick whichever matches the conversation; the rest of the page is the same either way.

**For someone who has tried to open a childcare center.** You already know this is not one approval but a stack of them, run by agencies that do not coordinate on your timeline. This page is just an attempt to put the actual stack on one page — who you talk to, what they decide, what has to finish before the next thing can start, and where most centers actually get stuck. If anything below is wrong about how it really works, that is the part worth saying out loud.

**For someone who works in small-business permitting more broadly.** Childcare is a clean instance of a pattern: a state license layered on top of three or four city approvals (zoning, building/CO, fire, environmental health, business license), with no single agency owning the end-to-end timeline. The friction is rarely in any one of those — it is in the order they have to happen in, and in who is allowed to proceed before whom. This page traces that order for one city.

**For someone who has not seen this kind of thing before.** Opening a childcare center in most US cities requires roughly five separate yes-answers from agencies that do not share a queue. Each one has its own form, its own fees, and its own waiting list. None of them will tell you the others' timelines. This page lays the five out for one specific city — Denver — so the size and shape of the thing are visible.

## The order of operations

Some of these can run in parallel. Others cannot start until something else has finished. The trap is in the second column, not the first.

| Can run in parallel                                                                                                                                                                                          | Must wait on something else                                                                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Apply for the state CDEC child care center license through the Provider Hub.                                                                                                                                 | The Denver business license **will not issue** until a proper zone use permit is on file. An "Acknowledgement of Zone Use Permit" lets the application begin, but not finish.                      |
| Email Denver CPD Records to ask whether the address already has a zone use permit (allow up to 10 business days).                                                                                            | The Denver Department of Excise and Licensing's initial application review (target: 7 business days) starts only after a complete application with all required documents is submitted.            |
| If no existing zone use permit, contact Denver Zoning to apply (allow up to 10 business days for a response).                                                                                                | Required city inspections (Development Services / Denver Fire / DDPHE) are scheduled only after the city license review is complete.                                                               |
| Begin background checks for owners, on-site managers, and staff (CBI/FBI fingerprint and child abuse & neglect checks; processing typically 2-4 weeks).                                                      | The DDPHE health inspection is scheduled only **after** classrooms are set up and **both** the state child care license application and the city business license application have been submitted. |
| Begin the DDPHE Plan Review submission (floor plan, plumbing if applicable, food/beverage equipment cut sheets, radon results within the last 6 months, lead testing, written illness policy, illness logs). | If any construction or plumbing work is involved, a **full building plan review** must clear through CPD e-permits before the health inspection or final license issuance.                         |
| Register the entity (true name, ownership, organizational documents) in Denver's Permitting and Licensing Center.                                                                                            | A change of use or occupancy requires a **new Certificate of Occupancy** before the use can lawfully begin.                                                                                        |

## Where most centers actually get stuck

Three friction points appear repeatedly in the published process — not because any one agency is slow, but because of how the dependencies stack.

1. **Zoning gate at the city license.** The Denver business license will not issue without a proper zone use permit on file. Applicants can begin the application with an "Acknowledgement of Zone Use Permit," but the license itself is held until the zoning permit is finalized. If the address turns out not to be zoned for child care, the entire application is at risk and is not eligible for a refund. Source: [Denver new child care center application — Step 1](https://www.denvergov.org/Government/Agencies-Departments-Offices/Agencies-Departments-Offices-Directory/Business-Licensing/Business-licenses/Child-care/Child-care-center/New).

2. **Health inspection cannot be scheduled until the room is built and two other applications are filed.** DDPHE will not schedule the health inspection until classrooms are physically set up and the state child care license application **and** the city business license application have both been submitted. So a center with construction work in flight ends up holding rent on a finished space while waiting for the health inspection that is itself waiting on two other queues. Source: [Denver "Guide for New Child Care Provider Health Inspection" (DDPHE, 2023)](https://www.denvergov.org/files/assets/public/v/1/public-health-and-environment/documents/phi/howtoopen_childcarefacility_2023.pdf).

3. **Building plan review feeds Certificate of Occupancy, which feeds everything else.** If the project involves any construction or plumbing, a full building plan review must clear through CPD e-permits, and a change in occupancy or use cannot happen without Building Official approval and a new Certificate of Occupancy. Until the CO is in hand, the inspections that depend on a usable space cannot pass. Source: [Denver CPD building-codes admin policies, ADMIN-142](https://denver.prelive.opencities.com/files/assets/public/v/1/community-planning-and-development/documents/ds/building-codes/policies/admin-142.pdf).

The pattern: every late-stage approval depends on something earlier finishing in a different agency that does not see the rest of the queue.

## Honest timing and cost (Denver, as published)

Numbers below are pulled from the agencies' own published materials. They are not estimates.

- **State CDEC license:** initial license processing typically runs **60-90 days** from application to issuance, with a licensing visit occurring at an unannounced point in that window. ([CDEC, Child Care Licensing and Administration](https://cdec.colorado.gov/for-providers/child-care-licensing-and-administration))
- **Denver city business license review:** initial review target is **7 business days** for a complete application. Incomplete applications get a "notice of deficiency" and 14 days to fix. Applications close if not completed within 12 months. ([Denver new child care center application — Step 7](https://www.denvergov.org/Government/Agencies-Departments-Offices/Agencies-Departments-Offices-Directory/Business-Licensing/Business-licenses/Child-care/Child-care-center/New))
- **Denver city license fees, by capacity (application + license, each):** $25 for 1-8 children, $100 for 9-25 children, $200 for 26 or more children. Nonprofits may request a fee waiver on the license fee but still pay application and late fees. ([Denver new child care center application — Step 6](https://www.denvergov.org/Government/Agencies-Departments-Offices/Agencies-Departments-Offices-Directory/Business-Licensing/Business-licenses/Child-care/Child-care-center/New))
- **Background checks:** Colorado CBI/FBI fingerprint checks run roughly $49.50 per person; child abuse & neglect checks roughly $35 per person; processing typically 2-4 weeks. ([CDEC Background Checks](https://cdec.colorado.gov/background-checks))
- **DDPHE health inspection visit:** typically **60-120 minutes** depending on number of classrooms; scheduled only after the room is set up and both license applications are submitted. ([DDPHE health-inspection guide](https://www.denvergov.org/files/assets/public/v/1/public-health-and-environment/documents/phi/howtoopen_childcarefacility_2023.pdf))
- **Zoning response windows:** Denver CPD Records and Zoning each request **up to 10 business days** to respond to inquiries. ([Denver new child care center application — Step 1](https://www.denvergov.org/Government/Agencies-Departments-Offices/Agencies-Departments-Offices-Directory/Business-Licensing/Business-licenses/Child-care/Child-care-center/New))

## What is _not_ in this one-pager

This page deliberately leaves out:

- Subsidy and federal-funding eligibility (CCDF/CCDBG, Head Start, the Colorado Universal Preschool program). Different track, different agencies.
- Staffing ratios, qualifications, training hours, and curriculum requirements. These are real and load-bearing, but they live in the [CDEC rules and regulations](https://cdec.colorado.gov/for-providers/rules-and-regulations) and the licensing rule book, not in the one-page flow.
- Quality-rating systems (Colorado Shines).
- Accreditation through national bodies (NAEYC and similar).
- Tax registration, employer-of-record questions, workers' compensation, and unemployment insurance.
- The state administrative-code text itself (Colorado Code of Regulations, Title 12, child-care rules). The flow above tells you who decides what; the rules tell you what the answer has to be.
